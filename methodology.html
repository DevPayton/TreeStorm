How we score tree damage

A weather app says "60 mph winds." We tell you if trees are coming down.

Wind speed alone doesn't fell a tree. The ground does. Here's the difference between a storm alert and a tree-damage alert — and why we measure something nobody else does.

What goes into the score

Two signals, pulled fresh for every storm.

SIGNAL 01

Wind, tuned for trees

We read the real gust speed from the active storm warning — not a city-wide forecast. And we score it against tree thresholds, not roof thresholds. Limbs start dropping around 25 mph; trees come down past 40.

Source: National Weather Service storm-based warnings, with the gust speed tagged on each one.
SIGNAL 02

Soil saturation

We add up the last 14 days of rainfall over the exact storm footprint. Saturated soil loosens its grip on roots — so the same wind that snaps a limb on dry ground uproots the whole tree on wet ground.

Source: radar-estimated rainfall (MRMS), measured over the storm's actual footprint — not a guess.

On dry ground, it takes a strong wind to pull a tree. On saturated ground, a moderate wind does it — which is exactly the job a plain weather alert would tell you to ignore.

What this is — and what it isn't.

We're not meteorologists, and we don't pretend to be. We don't forecast storms or chase hail. We watch the National Weather Service for you, and we translate raw storm data into one thing tree contractors actually need: where the tree damage is, ranked by how bad.

The soil-saturation layer is the part nobody else does. The big storm-data companies are built for roofers chasing hail — they think about shingles, not roots. We think about the ground, because that's what decides whether a storm leaves you a pile of branches or a yard full of removal jobs.

It's not a crystal ball. A score is a starting point that puts your best ZIPs at the top of the list so you knock the right doors first. The faster you move, the more of those jobs you close.
